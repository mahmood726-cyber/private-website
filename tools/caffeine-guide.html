The Caffeine Experiment

A two-week protocol to find out, with your own data, whether caffeine is a trigger for your palpitations.

Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors in your heart. In some people, this triggers palpitations. In others, it has no effect. The only way to know is to test it carefully. This guide walks you through the two-week caffeine experiment from your OpenPalp booklet.

The Two-Week Protocol

You may feel tired, headachy, or irritable. This is normal caffeine withdrawal.

These symptoms typically peak at 24–48 hours and then improve.

Record in your booklet
  • Energy levels (morning and evening)
  • Headache severity (0–10)
  • Palpitation frequency
Tip

Stay well hydrated. Decaf tea or coffee can help with the ritual.

Most withdrawal symptoms settle by day 3–4.

Pay attention to your heart rhythm. Are palpitations less frequent? Less noticeable?

Record in your booklet
  • Palpitation frequency (how many episodes per day)
  • Sleep quality
  • Mood
  • Kardia readings
Tip

This is the key observation window. Record everything.

Have ONE cup of your usual caffeinated drink.

Note exactly when you drink it and then observe for 4–6 hours.

Record in your booklet
  • Time of caffeine intake
  • Any palpitations (time and duration)
  • How they compare to your caffeine-free days
Tip

If palpitations return within hours, caffeine is likely a trigger for you.

If symptoms returned with caffeine: you have your answer. Consider reducing or eliminating it.

If no change: caffeine may not be your trigger. You can reintroduce gradually.

Either way, you now have data rather than guesswork.

Record in your booklet
  • Your decision
  • Ongoing palpitation diary

Science note

Some people have genetic variations (CYP1A2 slow metabolisers) that mean caffeine stays in their system much longer. If you notice palpitations within an hour of drinking coffee, you may be a slow metaboliser. This is not dangerous — but it means even moderate caffeine can trigger symptoms.
