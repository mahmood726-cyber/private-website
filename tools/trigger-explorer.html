Trigger Explorer

Understanding what sets off your palpitations is one of the most powerful things you can do. Explore the eight most common triggers, what happens in your body, and what you can try.

The Attention Loop

Before exploring individual triggers, it helps to understand the cycle that amplifies them all.

Understanding this loop is the first step to breaking it. Once you recognise the cycle, you can interrupt it with breathing, distraction, or reframing.

Eight Common Triggers

Click any card to explore what happens in your body, what you might notice, what to try, and where to record it in your programme booklet.

What happens

Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors in the heart. Adenosine normally has a calming effect on heart cells. Without it, the cells become more excitable, which can trigger extra beats.

What you might notice

Palpitations within 30–60 minutes of drinking coffee or tea. May feel like fluttering, skipped beats, or a racing sensation.

What to try

The two-week caffeine experiment (Week 3 of your programme). Remove caffeine for 5 days, then reintroduce.

Booklet reference

Record in your daily diary and Week 3 trigger log.

What happens

Alcohol has a complex effect. During drinking, it can directly irritate heart cells. After drinking, the withdrawal effect (even from small amounts) increases sympathetic nervous system activity. Dehydration and electrolyte shifts add further stress.

What you might notice

Palpitations during drinking or — more commonly — the morning after. Even one or two drinks can trigger episodes in sensitive people.

What to try

Track alcohol intake in your diary. Try a two-week alcohol-free period and compare your palpitation frequency.

Booklet reference

Record in your daily diary.

What happens

Sleep deprivation increases sympathetic (fight-or-flight) nervous system activity and reduces heart rate variability. Circadian disruption — irregular sleep times — compounds the effect. The UK Biobank study of 403,000 people linked disrupted sleep to increased arrhythmia risk.

What you might notice

More frequent or more noticeable palpitations after a bad night. Often worse in the late morning.

What to try

The sleep anchor experiment (Week 4). Set a fixed wake time for two weeks, including weekends.

Booklet reference

Record sleep quality daily. Use Week 4 sleep section.

What happens

Stress triggers adrenaline release, which increases heart rate and can cause extra beats. The attention loop amplifies this: noticing your heart → anxiety → more adrenaline → more palpitations.

What you might notice

Palpitations during stressful events, presentations, arguments, or anxious moments. Also common after the stress has passed (the adrenaline crash).

What to try

The 4–6 breathing technique directly counters the adrenaline response. Practise it daily, not just during episodes.

Booklet reference

Note stress levels in your daily diary.

What happens

When blood volume drops, the heart compensates by beating faster. This can trigger palpitations, especially in combination with other triggers like caffeine or exercise.

What you might notice

Palpitations on hot days, after exercise, or when you have not drunk enough water. Often accompanied by lightheadedness.

What to try

Aim for 1.5–2 litres of water daily. More in hot weather or after exercise.

Booklet reference

Note fluid intake in your daily diary.

What happens

A full stomach presses against the diaphragm, which sits next to the heart. Digestion also activates the vagus nerve, which can paradoxically trigger palpitations. Alcohol with a large meal compounds the effect.

What you might notice

Palpitations within 30–60 minutes of a large meal, especially in the evening.

What to try

Eat smaller, more frequent meals. Avoid lying down immediately after eating.

Booklet reference

Note meal size and timing in your daily diary.

What happens

During exercise, your heart rate naturally increases (sinus tachycardia). This is normal. However, some arrhythmias are triggered specifically by exercise. The key distinction: palpitations during gentle activity are usually benign; palpitations only during intense exercise need assessment.

What you might notice

Awareness of a fast or strong heartbeat during or immediately after exercise.

What to try

Record when exercise-related palpitations happen. If they only occur during intense exercise, mention this at your appointment.

Booklet reference

Record in Week 5 movement section.

What happens

Oestrogen and progesterone fluctuations can affect heart rhythm. Palpitations are common around menstruation, during perimenopause, and in pregnancy. Thyroid dysfunction (both overactive and underactive) is another hormonal cause.

What you might notice

Palpitations that follow a monthly pattern, or that started around menopause. Thyroid-related palpitations are often accompanied by weight changes, fatigue, or heat/cold intolerance.

What to try

Track palpitations against your menstrual cycle if relevant. If you suspect thyroid involvement, mention it at your appointment — a simple blood test can check.

Booklet reference

Note in your daily diary. Discuss at your appointment.
