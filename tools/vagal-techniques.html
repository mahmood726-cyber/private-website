Vagal Techniques — Step by Step

These techniques stimulate the vagus nerve, which can slow your heart rate during a palpitation episode. They work best when used early — as soon as you notice symptoms. Practise them when you feel well so they are familiar when you need them.

1

The Valsalva Manoeuvre

Pressure-based vagal activation

  1. 1 Sit down and take a deep breath in
  2. 2 Close your mouth and pinch your nose
  3. 3 Bear down — as if straining on the toilet
  4. 4 Hold for 10–15 seconds — start the timer when you begin bearing down
    15

    Hold for 15 seconds
  5. 5 Release suddenly and breathe normally
  6. 6 Wait 30 seconds. If palpitations continue, you can try again.
⚠
Do NOT use this technique if you have: glaucoma or eye disease, a known heart valve problem, or have been told to avoid straining.
How it works

Bearing down increases pressure in your chest, which stimulates the vagus nerve. The sudden release causes a reflex slowing of the heart.

2

Cold Water Technique

Dive reflex activation

  1. 1 Fill a bowl or basin with cold water (not ice)
  2. 2 Take a breath in and hold it
  3. 3 Submerge your face in the water for 10–20 seconds — start the timer as your face enters the water
    15

    Hold for 15 seconds
  4. 4 Lift your face out and breathe normally
No bowl available? Hold a cold flannel, cold pack, or bag of frozen peas against your forehead and cheeks for 20–30 seconds. This achieves a similar effect.
⚠
Not suitable if you have: Raynaud syndrome or severe cold sensitivity. Do not use ice water.
How it works

Cold water on the face triggers the mammalian dive reflex — an automatic response that slows the heart. It is one of the fastest vagal techniques.

3

The 4-6 Breathing Technique

Foundation technique — daily practice

This is the foundation technique of the OpenPalp programme. Inhale for 4 seconds, exhale for 6 seconds. The longer exhale activates the vagus nerve.

Breathe

Practise this twice daily — not just during episodes. A meta-analysis of 31 studies (1,133 people) found measurable heart rhythm effects within a single session.

The slow exhale amplifies cardiac vagal tone by extending the parasympathetic phase of each respiratory cycle. Regular practice lowers your resting heart rate variability baseline over weeks.

Read the evidence behind the 4-6 technique on the main site.

When to Use These Techniques

  1. 1 Feel palpitations starting → sit or lie down
  2. 2 Try slow breathing first (4-6 pattern, 1–2 minutes)
  3. 3 If palpitations persist → try Valsalva or cold water
  4. 4 Record on Kardia after the episode settles
  5. 5 If palpitations do not stop within 30 minutes, or you feel unwell → call 111 or attend A&E

This tool is for educational purposes only and is intended for use within the OpenPalp programme by patients who have been assessed as suitable. It does not replace medical advice. If you are unsure whether any of these techniques are appropriate for you, consult your cardiologist or GP before using them.
