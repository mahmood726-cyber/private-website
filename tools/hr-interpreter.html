Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

HRV measures the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats, expressed in milliseconds. Select which metric your device reports, then enter the value. Most consumer wearables report RMSSD.

Important notes about HRV

  • HRV varies enormously between individuals. Your trend over weeks matters far more than any single number.
  • Morning readings taken immediately after waking are the most consistent.
  • Your Kardia app may not display HRV. If it does, record it in your booklet.
  • HRV decreases with age — this is normal.

Heart rate variability is the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. Unlike heart rate, which counts beats per minute, HRV measures the subtle differences between each beat — typically expressed in milliseconds. Higher variability is generally associated with better health, adaptability, and resilience to stress. A perfectly regular heartbeat (zero variability) would actually be a warning sign, not a reassuring one.
Low HRV is associated with increased sympathetic (fight-or-flight) tone and reduced parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) activity. This imbalance can make the heart more reactive to triggers like caffeine, stress, and poor sleep — which are common drivers of ectopic beats and palpitations. The 4–6 breathing technique, regular exercise, and good sleep all improve HRV over time, which is one reason the OpenPalp programme includes all three. Tracking your HRV alongside your symptom diary can reveal which lifestyle factors matter most for your heart.
Many factors influence your HRV reading: sleep quality and duration, psychological stress, caffeine intake, alcohol, exercise load, age, illness, and medications (particularly beta-blockers and antidepressants). Even your posture changes it — lying down gives a higher reading than sitting. This is why consistency in measurement timing matters so much. Taking your reading in the same position, at the same time each morning, is the only way to track your true trend rather than measurement noise.
RMSSD (Root Mean Square of Successive Differences) measures short-term variability between adjacent beats. It reflects parasympathetic nervous system activity and is the metric most commonly reported by consumer wearables such as Apple Watch, Garmin, and Whoop. It is the preferred metric for daily tracking.

SDNN (Standard Deviation of NN intervals) captures overall variability over a longer recording — typically 24 hours. It reflects total autonomic tone, both sympathetic and parasympathetic. It is more commonly used in clinical 24-hour Holter recordings than in consumer devices. If your device reports SDNN, it is usually from a shorter interval than 24 hours, so interpret the number cautiously.
